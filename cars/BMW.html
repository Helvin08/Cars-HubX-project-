BMW History:



Let's delve into the main BMW series and some of their significant generations:

The Foundation: Pre-War Era

BMW's journey into automobile manufacturing began in the late 1920s.

Early Foundations (1929-1934)

  • BMW 3/15 PS (Dixi) (1929-1932): BMW's first foray into automobiles. Based on the Austin Seven, it was crucial for establishing BMW as a car manufacturer. It was a small, affordable car that helped BMW navigate the economic challenges of the time.
  • BMW 3/20 PS (1932-1934): This marked BMW's first independently designed car. It featured a 782 cc four-cylinder engine and showcased BMW's engineering ambitions beyond simply licensing existing designs.
  • BMW 303 (1933-1934): A pivotal model, the 303 introduced BMW's iconic twin-kidney grille and their first straight-six engine (1.2L). This engine was smoother and more powerful than the previous four-cylinders, hinting at BMW's future focus on refined performance.

Pre-War Golden Era (1934-1941)

  • BMW 315 & 319 (1934-1937): These models further developed the straight-six engine lineage with larger displacements (1.5L and 1.9L respectively). They offered improved performance and more luxurious features compared to the earlier models.
  • BMW 326 (1936-1941): A larger and more luxurious model, the 326 featured a sophisticated chassis and a smooth 2.0L straight-six engine. It was a significant step towards BMW's reputation for combining sportiness with elegance.
  • BMW 327 (1937-1941): The stylish coupe and convertible versions of the 326, showcasing BMW's design flair. These models are highly sought after by collectors today.
  • BMW 328 (1936-1940): A legendary sports car that cemented BMW's sporting credentials. Its advanced engine technology and lightweight design made it incredibly successful in motorsports, including the Mille Miglia.
  • BMW 335 (1939-1941): A larger, more luxurious model with a powerful 3.5L straight-six engine. It was known for its smooth ride and advanced features for the time.

Post-War Recovery and the "New Class" (1950s - 1970s)

BMW's journey to rebuild and reinvent itself after World War II.

Post-War Revival (1952-1965)

  • BMW 501 & 502 (1952-1962): Known as the "Baroque Angels," these were luxurious and elegant sedans powered by V8 engines (the 502 being Germany's first post-war V8). While beautiful, they were expensive and didn't achieve high sales volumes, putting BMW in a precarious financial position.
  • BMW 600 & 700 (1957-1965): These small, unconventional cars (the 600 was a stretched Isetta microcar) helped BMW survive the financial difficulties of the late 1950s by offering affordable transportation.

The New Class (1962-1977)

  • BMW 1500, 1600, 1800, 2000: This series was a turning point for BMW. These modern, sporty sedans with their independent suspension, overhead camshaft engines, and stylish design were commercially successful and established BMW's image as a manufacturer of sporty, premium sedans.
  • BMW 2002: A two-door version of the 1600/2000 that became particularly iconic for its performance and handling, setting the stage for BMW's future sporting sedans.

The Numbered Series Era (1970s - Present)

The Modern BMW Era

This is where BMW's modern naming convention truly took hold.

BMW 5 Series

  • E12 (1972-1981): The first generation of the 5 Series, establishing the template for the modern sporty executive sedan.
  • E28 (1981-1988): Introduced anti-lock brakes (ABS) and the first M5 (E28 M5).
  • E34 (1988-1996): Known for its refined handling and advanced technology. Offered in sedan and Touring (wagon) body styles.
  • E39 (1995-2003): Widely regarded as a benchmark in its class for its balance of comfort, handling, and performance.
  • E60/E61 (2003-2010): A more controversial design but packed with technology. Offered in sedan and Touring.
  • F10/F11 (2010-2016): A return to a more traditional and elegant design.
  • G30/G31 (2017-Present): The current generation, continuing the 5 Series tradition of blending luxury and sportiness with advanced technology.

BMW 3 Series

  • E21 (1975-1983): The first generation of the 3 Series, replacing the 02 Series and becoming BMW's entry-level model. Initially only available as a two-door sedan.
  • E30 (1982-1994): A hugely popular and iconic generation, available in two-door sedan, four-door sedan, convertible, and Touring body styles. Also spawned the first M3 (E30 M3), a legendary sports car.
  • E36 (1990-2000): A more modern and aerodynamic design, introduced the hatchback (Compact) and coupe body styles to the 3 Series lineup.
  • E46 (1998-2006): Another highly acclaimed generation, known for its balanced handling and refined driving experience.
  • E90/E91/E92/E93 (2005-2013): The first 3 Series generation to widely utilize turbocharged engines. Offered in sedan, Touring, coupe, and convertible.
  • F30/F31/F34/F35 (2012-2019): The sedan and Touring (F30/F31) were joined by the Gran Turismo (F34) with a hatchback design, and a long-wheelbase sedan for the Chinese market (F35). The coupe and convertible were spun off into the 4 Series.
  • G20/G21 (2019-Present): The current generation, emphasizing technology, connectivity, and a more dynamic driving experience.

BMW 7 Series

  • E23 (1977-1986): BMW's first large luxury sedan, establishing its presence in the executive car market.
  • E32 (1986-1994): Introduced more advanced technology and the first V12 engine in a post-war German car.
  • E38 (1994-2001): Considered by many to be a design classic, known for its elegant styling and sophisticated features.
  • E65/E66 (2001-2008): A controversial design that marked a significant shift in BMW's styling direction and iDrive infotainment system.
  • F01/F02 (2008-2015): A more conservative design approach with continued technological advancements.
  • G11/G12 (2015-Present): The current generation, featuring lightweight construction, advanced driver-assistance systems, and luxurious appointments.

BMW 6 Series & 8 Series

These series have seen various iterations and body styles over the years, representing BMW's grand tourers and luxury coupes/convertibles.

  • E24 6 Series (1976-1989): A stylish and sporty coupe based on the 5 Series platform.
  • E31 8 Series (1990-1999): A technologically advanced and luxurious grand tourer with V8 and V12 engine options.
  • E63/E64 6 Series (2003-2010): A more modern interpretation of the 6 Series, available as a coupe and convertible.
  • F12/F13/F06 6 Series (2011-2018): Included coupe, convertible, and Gran Coupe (four-door coupe) body styles.
  • G32 6 Series Gran Turismo (2017-Present): A more practical, hatchback-style grand tourer.
  • G14/G15/G16 8 Series (2018-Present): A revival of the 8 Series as a luxurious and high-performance coupe, convertible, and Gran Coupe.

BMW 1 Series & 2 Series

Representing BMW's compact offerings.

  • E81/E82/E87/E88 1 Series (2004-2013): The first generation of the 1 Series, available as a three-door hatchback, five-door hatchback, coupe, and convertible, and notably featured rear-wheel drive in a compact segment.
  • F20/F21 1 Series (2011-2019): The second generation 1 Series hatchbacks, continuing with rear-wheel drive.
  • F22/F23 2 Series (2014-Present): The coupe and convertible versions of the 1 Series were spun off into the 2 Series, maintaining rear-wheel drive.
  • F45/F46 2 Series Active Tourer/Gran Tourer (2014-Present): Front-wheel drive MPV models.
  • F44 2 Series Gran Coupe (2019-Present): A stylish four-door coupe with front-wheel drive.
  • G42 2 Series Coupe (2021-Present): The latest generation 2 Series Coupe, retaining rear-wheel drive.
  • U10/U11 1 Series (2022/2024-Present): The latest generation 1 Series hatchback, now with front-wheel drive.

BMW X Series (SUVs & Crossovers)

  • E53 X5 (1999-2006): BMW's first SUV, emphasizing sporty handling on-road.
  • E83 X3 (2003-2010): A more compact SUV.
  • E70 X5 (2006-2013): The second generation X5, offering more space and luxury.
  • E71 X6 (2008-2014): Pioneered the "Sports Activity Coupe" (SAC) design.
  • F25 X3 (2010-2017): The second generation X3, offering improved refinement.
  • F26 X4 (2014-2018): The coupe version of the X3 (SAC).
  • F15 X5 (2013-2018): The third generation X5.
  • F16 X6 (2014-2019): The second generation X6.
  • G01 X3 (2017-Present): The current generation X3.
  • G02 X4 (2018-Present): The current generation X4.
  • G05 X5 (2018-Present): The current generation X5.
  • G06 X6 (2019-Present): The current generation X6.
  • G07 X7 (2019-Present): BMW's flagship luxury SUV.
  • U11 X1 (2022-Present): The latest generation X1, now also available in electric form (iX1).
  • U06 X2 (2023-Present): The latest generation X2, also available in electric form (iX2).
  • iX3 (2020-Present): Electric version of the X3.
  • iX (2021-Present): A dedicated electric SUV.

BMW Z Series (Roadsters)

  • Z1 (1989-1991): A quirky and innovative roadster with unique drop-down doors.
  • E36/7 Z3 (1995-2002): A more mainstream roadster that gained popularity. Also available as a coupe (Z3 M Coupe "Clown Shoe").
  • E85/E86 Z4 (2002-2008): The successor to the Z3, available as a roadster and coupe.
  • E89 Z4 (2009-2016): A retractable hardtop roadster.
  • G29 Z4 (2018-Present): The current generation Z4, developed in collaboration with Toyota (Supra).

BMW i Series (Electric Vehicles)

  • i3 (2013-2022): A revolutionary electric city car with a carbon fiber reinforced plastic (CFRP) body.
  • i8 (2014-2020): A futuristic plug-in hybrid sports car.
  • iX (2021-Present): A flagship electric SUV.
  • i4 (2021-Present): An electric Gran Coupe.
  • iX1 (2022-Present): Electric version of the X1.
  • i7 (2022-Present): Electric version of the 7 Series.
  • i5 (2023-Present): Electric version of the 5 Series.
  • iX2 (2024-Present): Electric version of the X2.

M Models

High-performance versions of many of the above series, developed by BMW M GmbH (formerly BMW Motorsport). These models feature more powerful engines, upgraded suspension, aerodynamic enhancements, and distinctive styling.
